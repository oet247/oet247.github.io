## Introduction
“How do I play neutral, please save me!” is a question that is on a lot of Your Only Move Is Hustle players’ minds. I was also facing this conundrum in late 2024 and Eternum heroically came to my rescue by providing a very simple explanation that I base my entire understanding of the concept on, and which I still, to this day, think is the best entry point to learning effective neutral play. This document aims to streamline Eternum’s initial ideas and further expand on them, with contributions from Bargun, Nova, and me, to create a (hopefully) concise neutral framework.

Prerequisites: ZXavier's Your Only Guide To Hustle, experience with the game and the specific mechanics of the cast.

## Win conditions 
The end goal of neutral in YOMI is getting a combo started. In Albanian neutral theory, however, we look for the situations that precede hitting the opponent - we call these win conditions. The basic premise of this framework is that both players have their own distinct win conditions they strive towards. Thus, your first step to playing neutral well is setting up the right goals. The most basic example of a win condition to think about is where you and your opponent want to be situated, i.e. positioning.

A common pitfall many players fall into is to tunnel vision on themselves and what they want to do, but you also need to consider that there is an opponent on the other side of the screen with just as much going on in their head. Instead of focusing on your character’s strengths and weaknesses in a vacuum, think about the areas in which the opponent’s character excels and struggles - this way, you can avoid going into doomed situations. For example, Ninja has a relatively strong close range, which you want to exploit as much as possible against Wizard and Cowboy, who are weaker, but you don’t want to play into Robot and Mutant’s close range as often, since they are just as strong as you there.

## Move utility
Following from this, each move you do in neutral should be aimed at one of two things - preventing the opponent from achieving their goal or furthering your own. Conversely, the opponent also wants to work towards their own respective win conditions and to stop you from reaching yours. Actions that accomplish both of these at the same time are always remarkably good. Any move that does not fall into these categories should be avoided at all costs. If it serves no utility to your goals or thwarting the opponent’s, or even worse, if it makes the opponent’s job easier, then it is simply a waste of a turn. Now, we need a method to measure the value of different options and choose the best one, and this is where the following concepts come into play.

## Calculation
Calculation is all about culling useless moves and thinking about the possible gamestates that can pan out depending on both players’ choices. The first step is to go through each move one by one and try to find a reason why it’s not good. A lot of them can be quickly eliminated from consideration - some are obvious, like attacks that can’t hit the opponent in the first place, but others are less so. Moves that your opponent is likely to directly punish or ones that put you in dangerous situations down the line, you’ll be able to start catching more quickly with experience.

The next step is “Seeing the Future” - make a mental simulation of the different outcomes that can occur depending on what you select from the remaining moves. Think about how they affect the gamestate, how both players’ positioning will change, what resources are being gained or lost, etc. You will very often need to imagine what could happen a few turns in advance to accomplish this, which requires a good grasp of how the game functions.

## Evaluation heuristics
Now that you have calculated the possible outcomes of a situation, it’s time to use evaluation heuristics to rate the value of those outcomes. This is where I want to introduce the two concepts that bring everything together: threats and tempo.

### Threats
Let’s divide moves into four categories: hard threats, soft threats, movement, and defence.
Hard threats are active hitboxes created by setup moves. It takes at least a turn before they can threaten the opponent, but once you’ve spent the time setting them up, you can do other actions while having a hitbox out - they can attack for you or cover your sorry ass from getting hit. Think active bullets, magic dart, shuriken.
Soft threats are hitboxes that can potentially hit the opponent, i.e. most attacks. Unlike hard threats, you hit the opponent on the same turn - they are not telegraphed. This means neutral wins that don’t occur because of clear mistakes (i.e. running into a hard threat) are always due to cashing out on a soft threat that the opponent disrespected.
Movement allows the player to position themself around the threats and create threats of their own. Hard threats force movement since they can’t be avoided without the use of certain character-specific tools (e.g. projectile invulnerability). Unlike hard ones, soft threats encourage movement but don’t necessarily force it - you can disrespect them if you so wish. Moving also enables your own soft threats.
Defensive options like blocking can also be used to defend relatively safely against soft threats, but unlike movement, they usually force you to stay in one spot, which leaves you susceptible to incoming hard threats.

It follows that good moves are those that allow you to set up threats or avoid the opponent’s effectively, either preemptively in the case of soft threats or by reacting in the case of hard threats.

### Tempo
A player gains tempo as they get closer to their win condition relative to the opponent. This is most clearly visible in mirror matches; for example, a Wizard with 2.5 meter has tempo over a Wizard with 1.5 meter, as they are closer to Orb. You can gain tempo in two ways: either via movement, to accomplish your positional goal/deny the opponent’s, or by creating a threat, thus forcing the opponent to waste a turn. 

If the opponent has enough threats/tempo, you will be forced on the defensive, having to respond to them until they either hit you or you manage to escape and start your own offence. Neutral starts with both players jostling for position until one gains enough tempo, at which point they force responses from the other. Thus, moves that let you gain tempo can snowball the game into your favour.

## Preparation
The information gained from repeatedly applying the ideas outlined above now enters your repertoire, and the process becomes effectively automatic - a part of your preparation. Unlike active calculation, preparation requires little additional mental effort beyond recalling previous interactions and positions. The clear advantage comes from how well this lets you perform in high-pressure situations, where completing the proper calculation procedure in full is impossible due to timer constraints or stress, which is extremely important for tournaments and competitive play as a whole. 

The drawback is that primarily relying on what you’ve already learned and not straying far from your established mould will lead to stagnation in your game. You need to periodically update your preparation by analysing positions and coming up with new ways to deal with them, either in actual games or singleplayer/replay reviews. It’s a simple loop: figure something out through calc -> it becomes a part of your prep -> that eventually becomes outdated -> improve it through calc, and the loop starts anew. 

The Platonic ideal of preparation is to have seen every possible gamestate at least once and to be able to recall that information at will. This is not realistically possible, both due to the sheer complexity of the game and the limits of human memory. There is, however, a compromise: learn as much as you need to be able to make highly educated guesses about any gamestate you could find yourself in. As previously stated, being able to foresee the next n turns in your mind is a key aspect of calculation and, therefore, of the entire strategy of YOMI; accurate and extensive preparation is the most effective way to minimise your margin of error in these simulations.
